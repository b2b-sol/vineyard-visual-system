# Vineyard Workflow Discovery for a Vineyard Information System

## Executive workflow summary

A commercial wine-grape business with multiple estate blocks and contract-managed or contract-grower fruit is best understood as a coordination system, not just a farming system. The most consequential work is usually not the individual field task itself, but the chain that links observation, planning, crew assignment, materials, timing, quality targets, compliance constraints, harvest logistics, and financial reconciliation. Public role descriptions for vineyard managers, viticulturists, grower-relations staff, and estate managers consistently place these mid-level roles at the center of day-to-day operations: they translate annual plans into seasonal tasks, supervise crews and contractors, manage irrigation, crop protection, and harvest activities, maintain records, coordinate with winemaking, and communicate with buyers, growers, and leadership. In smaller or more informal organizations, the same person often combines field supervision, administrative control, and relationship management rather than handing them off cleanly across departments. citeturn6view0turn6view1turn6view2turn37view0

The operational middle layer matters because vineyard work is both seasonal and event-driven. A scouting note about mildew risk, a change in weather, a restricted-entry interval, a contractor delay, a winery request for updated maturity data, or a truck timing issue can all force rapid replanning. University IPM and viticulture guidance shows how much vineyard work is triggered by growth stage and monitoring rather than by a static task list: delayed-dormancy and budbreak monitoring, weekly pest checks, mildew risk tracking, crop estimation before harvest, maturity sampling, and postharvest sanitation all recur within tight seasonal windows. At the same time, winery-facing sources show that growers are expected to provide timely crop estimates, maturity data, spray records, and delivery coordination, while wineries are expected to communicate specifications, notice of downgrades or rejection, and harvest timing with enough lead time for action. citeturn38view0turn27view2turn27view4turn8view0turn33view2turn33view3

Across regions, the same recurring friction appears: the vineyard business depends on a surprisingly fragile chain of records. Spray diaries, work activity records, irrigation notes, crop estimates, maturity results, weigh tickets, payroll data, and block identifiers are often created in one context and consumed in another. The operational burden increases sharply when the same block is named differently across grower, vineyard, winery, compliance, and accounting systems; Wine Australia’s interoperability work identifies row/block identification as a core point of failure because blocks are split, renamed, combined, or represented differently across supply-chain systems, making comparison and history difficult. The same burden appears in practice-oriented guidance from Australia and New Zealand, where spray diaries may differ by buyer and where certification requires annual submission, auditability, and block-level application detail. citeturn31view0turn31view1turn31view2turn31view3turn32view0

The most important workflow patterns for product discovery are therefore not “viticulture topics” in isolation. They are recurring coordination loops: scouting to action, plan to crew assignment, application to compliance record, crop estimate to winery forecast, maturity updates to pick decision, harvest to weigh-ticket reconciliation, and field activity to payroll and cost reporting. Those loops are where duplicate entry, informal communication, and avoidable rework accumulate. The evidence base for this report is strongest for documented requirements, public best-practice procedures, certification workflows, and role expectations; weakest for informal whiteboard-and-text-message behavior, which is reconstructed here as a reasonable composite inference where multiple sources imply the same coordination burden. citeturn38view0turn28view0turn28view1turn8view2turn25view1turn30view0

## Vineyard operating model

A broadly applicable vineyard operating model has five interacting layers. At the top, ownership, a general manager, or a combined CEO/GM sets acreage targets, capital and operating budgets, customer or winery relationships, and business risk posture. In the middle, the vineyard manager, assistant manager, viticulturist, grower-relations lead, and operations or compliance coordinators convert those plans into block-level actions. Below them, foremen, irrigators, scouts, mechanics, pesticide handlers, equipment operators, and harvest crew leaders execute the work. Parallel to all of this, office functions handle payroll, purchasing, invoice matching, certifications, insurance, and management reporting. Finally, outside the firm boundary, wineries, contract growers, labs, certifiers, county or state regulators, labor contractors, and custom harvesters continuously influence the workflow. Public job descriptions and certification programs repeatedly describe the manager layer as the place where field, office, and outside-party information converge. citeturn6view0turn6view1turn37view0turn37view1turn15view1turn31view4

The role boundaries are usually clear in theory and blurry in practice. A vineyard manager may own budgets, labor supervision, harvest coordination, and customer communication at once. A viticulturist may not carry formal line authority over crews, but still drives nutrition, pest, canopy, and crop-load decisions through recommendations and monitoring. A grower-relations role may spend harvest doing maturity sampling, map-book maintenance, vineyard visits, and laboratory coordination rather than traditional “sales” work. In smaller operations, one estate or vineyard manager may also run a sprayer, manage payroll inputs, maintain pest records, serve as winery liaison, and advise contract growers. This role overlap is not incidental; it is a defining feature of the workflow environment you are studying. citeturn6view2turn37view1turn37view0turn6view0

## Role-based workflow maps

The vineyard manager’s daily work is a mix of inspection, prioritization, dispatch, exception handling, and documentation. The public postings most useful for reconstructing this role describe responsibility for pruning, canopy work, irrigation, nutrition, pest and disease control, crop estimation, harvest, employee and contractor supervision, budget development, compliance, data capture, and reporting. In multi-site settings, the manager also travels between blocks, monitors weather and soil conditions, coordinates equipment maintenance, and collaborates with winemaking on crop loads and harvest timing. In hybrid estate-plus-grower settings, the manager may also market and sell fruit, track contracted tonnage and pricing, communicate fruit availability to buyers, and coordinate laboratory results and delivery logistics. citeturn6view0turn6view1turn37view0

The viticulturist’s workflow is narrower in title but broader in influence. Scheid’s viticulturist posting is especially revealing because it lists planning and budgeting, pruning and canopy operations, labor coordination, irrigation scheduling, nutrition programs validated with petiole analysis, pest-prevention programs, trial blocks, and direct work with the winemaking team. That is a classic influence-heavy role: the viticulturist often does not perform every operation personally, but they repeatedly interpret scattered inputs and convert them into recommendations that others execute. The decisions they make repeatedly include whether a observed issue is real or noise, whether the block is in balance, whether the canopy should be opened, whether nutrient or water adjustments are warranted, whether to recommend treatment, whether crop load will meet the style goal, and when the evidence is sufficient to revise the harvest forecast. citeturn6view2turn18view2turn35search1

Grower-relations work sits at the boundary between viticulture, procurement, quality assurance, and operations. The harvest-temp grower-relations posting from Delicato is valuable because it shows the frontline version of the work: monitoring grower vineyards for pests, diseases, fruit soundness, and cultural practices; collecting maturity samples during harvest; maintaining GPS units and map books; coordinating sample processing; and supporting reports, calendars, and inventory. Contract and industry guidance adds the rest of the loop: growers report progress and condition, often including brix, TA, and pH; wineries and growers cooperate to determine harvest dates even where final pick authority sits with the winery; and delivery instructions, containers, quality tolerances, downgrade processes, and payment terms must all reconcile back to the contract. citeturn37view1turn8view1turn8view0turn8view2

Operations coordination is often hidden inside other titles, but its work is visible everywhere. Someone has to book custom operators, ensure bins are available, confirm which block is being worked today, manage or request PPE and materials, get equipment repaired, push payroll-support records through on time, and make sure the records created in the field can be used later by compliance, management, and finance. The evidence is diffuse rather than explicit: Fenn Valley’s estate manager coordinates pest records, payroll, regulatory compliance, maintenance, grower liaison, and harvest logistics; SIP, SWNZ, CSWA, and LODI all require written plans, annual evidence, and corrective actions that somebody operationally maintained must assemble; and cost studies assume tracking of labor, custom work, materials, and equipment activity at a level detailed enough to support enterprise budgeting and cost analysis. citeturn37view0turn15view0turn15view1turn15view2turn31view4turn25view1

Compliance and sustainability personnel are best understood as workflow integrators, not mere record archivists. California Sustainable Winegrowing requires written action plans and annual measurement of specific resource inputs. LODI RULES organizes requirements across business management, human resources, ecosystem, soil, water, and pest management, with annual block-based certification and independent audits. SWNZ requires annual questionnaires, site management plans, full spray diaries, a biosecurity register, and periodic audits; where noncompliance appears, certification is withheld until corrective actions are completed. The common operational effect is that compliance work reaches back into ordinary vineyard tasks: which product was used, on which block, by whom, at what rate, and how that aligns with plan, law, and buyer requirements. citeturn15view0turn15view1turn15view2turn31view3turn31view4

## Detailed workflow cards and handoff maps

**Workflow card: seasonal work planning and work-order dispatch**

**Business purpose.** Convert annual production targets, current field conditions, and external constraints into executable block-level work. This is strongly supported by manager and viticulturist role descriptions, annual cost studies, and seasonal extension guidance; the “work order” label itself is often absent from public vineyard materials, so the reconstructed workflow below is a high-confidence composite rather than a directly documented universal SOP. citeturn6view0turn6view1turn6view2turn25view1turn38view0

**Trigger.** Seasonal stage change, scouting result, budgeted recurring work, weather forecast, winery request, certification requirement, or equipment issue. **Primary owner.** Vineyard manager. **Other participants.** Viticulturist, foreman, operations coordinator, contractor, compliance staff. **Inputs.** Block map, growth stage, work history, labor availability, equipment status, materials, restricted-entry or preharvest constraints where applicable. **Current channels.** Spreadsheets, reporting systems, software, maps, phone, email, and verbal direction are all evidenced; informal coordination beyond that is a reasonable inference. citeturn6view0turn37view0turn38view0turn28view1

**Typical sequence.** Inspect or receive a field signal; decide whether action is required; define the block, timing, method, crew or contractor, and required materials; check for competing priorities and restrictions; dispatch the task to a foreman or supervisor; field team executes; completion status, exceptions, and quantities used flow back to management; office-side records are updated for cost, payroll, compliance, and progress. **Approvals.** Often lightweight for routine work; stronger for pesticide applications, larger purchases, contractor engagement, certification-sensitive activities, or harvest. **Outputs and records.** Activity logs, labor allocation, materials use, completed or deferred status, and possibly purchase or contractor records. **Pain points.** Priority changes, incomplete block identity, poor closure on “done/not done,” and weak capture of actual labor, equipment time, or acreage covered. **Confidence.** Medium-high as a composite workflow. citeturn6view0turn36search19turn25view1turn28view2

**Workflow card: scouting to spray execution and compliance record**

**Business purpose.** Detect pest or disease pressure early enough to intervene effectively while protecting workers, meeting label and buyer requirements, and preserving auditability. This is one of the best-documented workflows in the evidence set. citeturn38view0turn28view0turn28view1turn31view2turn32view0

**Trigger.** Scheduled monitoring, trap result, weather-based disease risk, observed symptoms, or certification review. **Primary owner.** Viticulturist or crop-protection lead, with vineyard manager approval norms depending on organization. **Preconditions.** Accurate block identity, current regulations or buyer rules, available product and applicator, acceptable weather, no conflicting REI/PHI issue. **Step sequence.** Monitor and keep records; confirm pressure and treatment threshold; select material and method; verify calibration, wind, sensitive areas, PPE, REI, and PHI constraints; execute application; post or display required information; update spray diary and any jurisdictional report; follow up for efficacy. UC IPM explicitly calls for monitoring records, calibration, weather checks, REI/PHI review, application recording, and follow-up efficacy checks. EPA WPS requires worker protections, handler instructions, PPE, central posting, and access to application information. citeturn38view0turn28view0turn28view1

**Outputs and records.** Application date, location, product, rate, active ingredients, operator, method, target pest or disease, and timing relative to restrictions. SWNZ and seller-facing spray diary programs require detailed block- and operator-level entries; California reporting adds operator identification, site identification, and county submission structures. **Exceptions.** Wind, rain, unavailable product, worker presence in exclusion zones, incorrect diary entry, uncertified product, or late submission. **Workarounds.** Rescheduling, block splitting, alternate products, manual follow-up corrections, or separate buyer-specific diaries where common formats do not exist. **Risks if failure occurs.** Worker exposure, rejected fruit, lost certification, residue problems, failed audit, or inability to defend what happened later. **Confidence.** High. citeturn32view0turn31view3turn31view4turn28view2turn20search10

**Workflow card: irrigation scheduling and fertility adjustment**

**Business purpose.** Maintain vine balance, quality targets, and resource efficiency by translating plant and soil signals into water and nutrient actions. **Trigger.** Routine irrigation cycle, moisture or stress readings, weather, growth stage, heat event, or tissue-analysis result. **Primary owner.** Viticulturist or irrigation lead; vineyard manager coordinates labor and resources. **Inputs.** Soil moisture, evapotranspiration or site conditions, plant status, block history, nutrient sampling, recent applications, and target style or vigor. WSU’s regional pest plan describes soil-moisture and evapotranspiration monitoring as key components of precision water management, and vine balance guidance connects irrigation and fertilizer choices directly to canopy size, labor requirements, and fruit quality. citeturn35search11turn18view2

**Typical sequence.** Review recent block and weather conditions; inspect irrigation system or receive irrigator update; decide whether to increase, reduce, continue, or defer irrigation; if fertility adjustment is under consideration, review tissue or petiole results and recent nutrient inputs; instruct irrigator or fertigation/applicator crew; verify completion and note any malfunction or uneven delivery; update operating logs and, where relevant, sustainability metrics. **Outputs and records.** Water status notes, irrigation records, possibly water-use totals and nitrogen-use totals for certification or management reporting. **Pain points.** Limited visibility into actual delivery; mismatch between block reality and office assumptions; weak linkage between observed stress, applied water, and later quality or yield outcomes. **Confidence.** Medium-high; strong agronomic evidence, less direct public evidence for exact manager-office choreography. citeturn15view0turn35search11turn35search1

**Workflow card: crop estimation and maturity tracking to harvest-date decision**

**Business purpose.** Keep crop load, winery planning, labor, and harvest quality aligned. **Trigger.** Lag phase, véraison, preharvest windows, winery request, or visible block variation. **Primary owner.** Viticulturist or grower-relations role, with manager and winemaker participation. **Inputs.** Vines per block, bunches per vine, cluster weights, historical yield, berry or cluster samples, Brix, pH, TA, flavor and condition assessments, weather, labor availability, tank capacity. WSU, BC, Ohio, and contract sources all support this general structure. citeturn27view2turn27view3turn27view0turn27view4turn8view1

**Typical sequence.** Count and sample representative vines; update forecast; compare against style and capacity goals; decide whether crop adjustment is needed; as harvest nears, increase sample frequency; send results to winemaker, manager, grower, or buyer as relevant; negotiate or confirm desired pick date; turn the decision into labor, bin, truck, and winery schedule commitments. **Approvals.** Usually shared; many arrangements rely on communication and mutual agreement even where final pick timing sits with the winery. **Exceptions.** Rapid rot development, weather shifts, smoke, labor shortage, or a winery change in capacity or target. **Pain points.** Sample representativeness, inconsistent block IDs, stale results by the time they are communicated, and disputes when field and winery measurements differ. **Confidence.** High for the broad loop; medium for specific thresholds because they vary by region and buyer. citeturn27view0turn27view4turn8view1turn33view4

**Workflow card: harvest execution to weigh-ticket and payment reconciliation**

**Business purpose.** Move fruit from the correct block to the correct winery intake at the correct time and condition, then turn physical delivery into accurate commercial records. **Trigger.** Final pick authorization. **Primary owner.** Vineyard manager for estate fruit; grower-relations or grower plus winery intake for contract fruit. **Inputs.** Pick list, crew assignment, machine or hand-harvest choice, containers, trucking plan, block ID, sample IDs, winery slots, cooling or night-harvest strategy. BC guidance, Wine Australia’s vineyard-winery protocols, Ontario’s preharvest package, and contract guidance all converge on this being a coordination-heavy workflow. citeturn27view1turn33view3turn8view1turn8view2

**Typical sequence.** Confirm pick and delivery slot; stage bins or gondolas; brief harvest crew; harvest in agreed conditions; transport with block and load identification; weigh and sample at intake; accept, downgrade, or reject according to tolerances; retain and transmit weigh documentation; enter delivery data into buyer or industry system; reconcile actual delivered weight, quality results, and contract terms for settlement. Ontario’s eGrape workflow is especially concrete: the grower indicates grower number, variety, vineyard and block on the weigh bill, sends it with the truck, keeps a carbon copy, the processor enters the weigh bill within 72 hours, and the grower reviews the entry for accuracy. citeturn8view2

**Outputs and records.** Weigh tickets, quality results, delivery records, rejection or adjustment paperwork, harvest summary, payment or settlement inputs. **Exceptions.** Winery delays, hot fruit, MOG, rejected loads, wrong block on paperwork, changed tank availability, missed truck slot, or late-harvest quality deterioration. **Workarounds.** Temporary alternate delivery, emergency resorting, repricing or downgrade negotiation, manual correction of weigh entries, or seeking another buyer where notice arrives early enough. **Confidence.** High. citeturn8view0turn8view1turn8view2turn33view2

## Certification overlays, exception inventory, digital opportunity map, field research guide, and sources

Certified-organic workflows do not merely change field practices; they add administrative proof requirements around field history, buffer zones, maps, contamination prevention, equipment cleaning, transport cleaning, and how buffer-zone crop is handled. USDA guidance requires distinct field boundaries and sufficient buffers, keeps responsibility for contamination prevention with the organic operator even when contractors make the mistake, and provides optional but workflow-revealing forms such as equipment cleaning logs, clean-transport affidavits, and buffer-crop disposition records. In real workflow terms, organic farming adds more prechecks before purchase and application, more separation rules during field and transport operations, and more evidence that must survive inspection and audit. citeturn12view0turn12view1turn12view2

Biodynamic workflows add another layer, but one that mixes hard certification requirements with more variable practice. The strongest documented operational requirements come from Demeter standards: the farm must use biodynamic preparations, those preparations are inspected as part of annual certification, and at least one application of key preparations is required during conversion; the standards also maintain broader annual inspection and worker-rights commitments. Some biodynamic practices are formal and auditable, such as use of preparations and certification review; others are more farm-specific or philosophically interpreted. For software discovery, the practical implication is that biodynamic operations need scheduling, inventory, traceability, training, and inspection evidence for activities that conventional vineyard systems may not even record. citeturn13view1turn13view4turn12view3

A realistic exception and workaround inventory starts with the disruptions that repeatedly force people outside the normal path. Frost compresses decision-making into weather-watch windows and depends on inversion, system type, and on-site equipment readiness. Smoke risk creates urgent sampling, conditional harvest decisions, contract renegotiation pressure, and buyer hesitation, while California research shows managers still lack enough information for confident operational adjustments. Heat changes schedule design, hydration, supervision intensity, and safety observations, especially for new or returning workers. Harvest-time rejections or downgrades create immediate calls, alternate arrangements, repricing, and commercial disputes. Missing or late spray records can threaten acceptance of fruit or certification standing. Weak block identity forces manual matching across winery, grower, and compliance records. citeturn34view0turn34view1turn34view3turn29view3turn33view2turn33view4turn31view1
